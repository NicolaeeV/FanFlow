**Why it's not just Google Maps**


Google Maps ranks local results largely by prominence, which is heavily review-count driven (Google dropped Bayesian smoothing in 2017). So a chain with thousands of reviews structurally outranks a beloved 34-review taqueria — even at a worse star score. FanFlow re-ranks on three signals Maps doesn't:

**1. Bayesian rating — trusts a score in proportion to evidence:**


The 34-review taqueria (4.48) beats Taco Bell (3.71) — and a thin 5.0★/6-review place (4.30) does not top it. So it neither buries the low-review gem nor over-trusts the thin 5-star. That's the core differentiator vs Maps.

**2. Chain detection + penalty.** 

is_chain flags known brands (and any name at 3+ locations), applies an authenticity penalty, and the planner actively drops chains when real local options exist — the opposite of prominence ranking.

**3. Hidden-gem boost. Well-rated + locally-loved + under-reviewed gets an explicit bonus.**

Plus the match-day reasoning Maps doesn't do at all: route-vs-kickoff, halftime/post-rush timing, open-at-the-asked-hour, halal/diet as hard filters, multilingual.

Are we assisting mom-and-pop shops? Yes — that's the whole owner side.
The owner agent gives a small owner the toolkit a chain's corporate team has: a match-day demand forecast, staffing/inventory, Google Business Profile readiness, an honest Google Ads plan, multilingual copy, and a "here's why your gem sits low on Google and what's actually fixable" coach (it never promises a buyable rank). So FanFlow helps them two ways: surfaces them over chains to fans, and helps them get found when the crowd searches.

**Honest caveats**
The data has to actually contain the local spot — differentiation is only as good as coverage/freshness.
Chain detection is a heuristic (brand list + multi-location), not perfect.
